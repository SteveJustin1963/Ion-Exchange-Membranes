# grinding
**Detailed Resin Grinding Techniques for DIY Ion-Exchange Membrane Fabrication**

The core grinding step in Robert Karas' (@Rowow) open-source PVC-cement + ion-exchange-resin membrane recipe is critical for performance. The resin must be reduced to a **super fine powder** so that PVC binder fully encapsulates the particles, creating a homogeneous composite with good ion conductivity, low resistance, minimal crossover, and mechanical integrity. Coarse particles lead to poor film formation, higher resistance, or brittle/crumbly membranes.

### Why Fineness Matters
- Ion-exchange resin beads (typically 0.3–1.2 mm diameter in water softener form) are cross-linked polystyrene with functional groups (e.g., sulfonate for cation exchange).
- Grinding exposes and distributes these groups evenly in the PVC matrix.
- Too coarse → tortuous ion paths, higher ohmic drop, possible voids/gaps.
- Over-grinding (to dust-like) → excessive dust, static issues, but usually not a problem if kept slightly moist.

Target particle size (inferred from successful replications and analogous heterogeneous IEM literature): **<50–100 µm**, ideally talc- or flour-like consistency, with no visible grit when rubbed between fingers.

### Recommended Equipment & Techniques (Lab-Reproducible)
All methods below are derived from the original video, follow-up demonstrations by Karas/Rowow, and community replications (e.g., Sciencemadness, goldrefiningforum, YouTube builds). Use in order of increasing fineness/repeatability.

1. **Primary Method: High-Speed Spice/Grain/Blender Grinder** (Most Accessible & Recommended for Garage/Lab)
   - **Device**: Electric spice grinder, grain mill, or herb/coffee grinder rated for dry grinding (e.g., 150–300 W models on Amazon ~$20–50; examples: "high-speed grain grinder", "electric herb grinder 300g", "spice grinder stainless steel").
   - **Batch size**: 50–100 g resin beads at a time (overfilling reduces efficiency).
   - **Preparation**:
     - Start with **damp or slightly moist beads** (as supplied in water softener bags; do **not** oven-dry them fully — residual moisture ~10–20% helps prevent clumping and dust explosion risk).
     - If beads are bone-dry, lightly mist with distilled water (just enough to dampen without pooling) before grinding.
   - **Grinding protocol**:
     - Pulse in short bursts (5–10 seconds) to avoid overheating the motor/resin.
     - Grind in multiple cycles: 3–5 rounds of 20–30 seconds each, stopping to shake/stir contents.
     - Total time: 2–5 minutes per batch until powder feels silky and no beads remain intact.
     - Sieve optional: Pass through 100–200 mesh sieve (149–74 µm) to remove any remaining coarse bits; re-grind residue.
   - **Tips from replications**:
     - Work in small batches to prevent motor burnout.
     - Clean grinder immediately after (PVC cement will glue residue permanently).
     - If static builds (powder sticks to walls), add a tiny pinch of anti-static agent or lightly mist grinder interior.
   - **Expected result**: Fine, off-white/tan powder resembling powdered sugar or fine talc; slight clumping OK if broken up.

2. **Two-Stage Upgrade: Ball Mill + Finishing Grinder** (For Higher Consistency & Finer Powder)
   - **Step 1 – Coarse pre-grind**: Use ball mill (rock tumbler + ceramic/steel media) for 4–12 hours on damp beads → breaks beads into ~100–500 µm chunks.
   - **Step 2 – Finish**: Transfer to high-speed spice grinder for 1–3 minutes as above → achieves sub-50 µm particles.
   - **Why better**: More uniform size distribution; less heat buildup; higher throughput for larger batches.
   - Common setup: Cheap hobby rock tumbler (~$50) + 1/2" ceramic media.

3. **Alternative Low-Tech Options** (If No Electric Grinder)
   - Mortar & pestle → extremely tedious; only for tiny test batches (~5–10 g); finish with coffee grinder if possible.
   - Manual grain mill (e.g., hand-crank Corona-style) → better than mortar but still coarse; follow with spice grinder.

### Post-Grinding Handling & Storage (Critical!)
- **Do NOT let the powder dry out completely**: Dry powder loses ion-exchange capacity rapidly due to collapse of hydrated channels.
  - Store immediately in airtight container with a small humidity source (e.g., damp paper towel or wet sponge in separate compartment; aim for ~50–70% RH).
  - Many builders report: "If you crush/dry the resin fully, performance tanks — keep it slightly damp."
- Weigh powder quickly after grinding and mix with PVC cement ASAP (within hours) while still moist.
- If storing longer: Seal in zip-lock with desiccant packet removed or replaced with humidity packet.

### Common Pitfalls & Troubleshooting
- **Powder too coarse** → Membrane has high resistance (>10–20 Ω·cm²), low current in tests. Solution: Grind longer/more cycles or add ball-mill step.
- **Excessive dust/static** → Add trace moisture or grind in pulses.
- **Clumping during grinding** → Beads were too wet → dry slightly (air-dry 10–20 min) and retry.
- **Overheating** → Motor smells hot or powder darkens → shorter bursts + cooling breaks.
- **Yield loss** → Electrostatic cling → tap grinder sides vigorously; use anti-static brush.

### Quick Validation Test
After mixing 50:50 vol powder + PVC cement (e.g., Oatey clear) and brushing onto fiberglass:
- Dry 30–60 min (still slightly tacky/moist inside).
- Soak in 1 M NaCl or target electrolyte → should swell slightly but remain intact.
- Simple electrolysis test (e.g., CuSO₄ | membrane | NaCl with carbon electrodes) → current should flow preferentially one way, minimal gas crossover.

This grinding protocol is directly repeatable with ~$30–80 investment in a good grinder. Follow the "keep slightly damp" rule religiously — it's the single biggest factor in getting functional, low-resistance membranes comparable to commercial ones at <$1/yd². If you run into specific issues (e.g., resin brand differences), share details for further tweaks!

# mixing resin with pvc cememnt

**Detailed Mixing Technique: Ion-Exchange Resin Powder with PVC Cement**

This step is the heart of Robert Karas' (@Rowow) open-source DIY ion-exchange membrane fabrication. The goal is a thick, paint-like paste that fully impregnates fiberglass mesh reinforcement while distributing the finely ground cation-exchange resin (from water softener beads) homogeneously in a PVC binder matrix. The PVC cement (solvent-based, typically tetrahydrofuran/cyclohexanone) dissolves slightly into the resin surface for good adhesion without fully dissolving the cross-linked beads, creating a semi-rigid, ion-permeable composite upon solvent evaporation.

### Key Ratio & Rationale
- **Standard ratio**: **50:50 by volume** (powder : PVC cement) — this is the most commonly repeated and successful "sweet spot" from the original demonstrations.
  - Equivalent to roughly 1:1 volumetric parts (e.g., 50 mL powder + 50 mL cement).
  - In some tests/builds: adjustable 30–70% powder by volume (e.g., 10–70% resin powder), but **50% is optimal** for balancing conductivity (higher resin = better ion transport) vs. mechanical strength/film integrity (higher PVC = tougher, less brittle membrane).
  - Weight-based for repeatability: ~1:1.2–1.5 resin powder : PVC cement by mass (resin is denser; typical dry powder density ~0.7–0.8 g/cm³, PVC cement ~1.0–1.1 g/cm³). Weigh for precision if scaling batches.

### Materials
- Finely ground cation-exchange resin powder (from water softener beads, <50–100 µm, slightly damp as per prior grinding notes).
- **Clear PVC cement** (not purple primer, not CPVC cement — use standard all-purpose/clear "regular" or "medium" body PVC pipe cement, e.g., Oatey, Christy's Red Hot Blue Glue, or equivalent hardware-store brands). Avoid "heavy duty" variants with too much filler.
- Disposable plastic or paper cup (PVC cement bonds permanently to glass/metal — do not use those).
- Stirring tool: wooden stick, plastic spoon, or spatula.
- Optional: digital scale (for weight ratio), small whisk or mini electric mixer for uniformity.

### Step-by-Step Mixing Protocol (Lab-Reproducible)
1. **Prepare workspace** — Ventilated area or fume hood (strong solvent fumes — THF is volatile and neurotoxic in high exposure). Wear nitrile gloves; eye protection recommended.

2. **Measure components**:
   - Scoop or weigh resin powder into cup first (e.g., 50 mL or 40 g).
   - Add equal volume of PVC cement slowly while stirring (pour in stages to avoid lumps).

3. **Initial mixing**:
   - Stir vigorously with stick/spatula immediately — mixture starts as thick slurry.
   - Continue mixing 1–2 minutes until uniform. It will be **thick, paste-like** (consistency of heavy paint or peanut butter; spreads but holds shape).
   - Break up any dry pockets or clumps — powder should be fully wetted/coated. If too dry/thick → add tiny amounts of cement (5–10% more max). If too runny → add more powder.

4. **Rest & degas (optional but helpful)**:
   - Let sit 1–2 minutes — allows bubbles to rise and solvents to penetrate resin slightly.
   - Remix briefly — improves homogeneity.

5. **Application timing**:
   - Use immediately (within 5–15 minutes) — cement begins to thicken as solvents evaporate.
   - Work quickly once applied to mesh — wet paste flows into weave easily.

### Critical Tips & Troubleshooting
- **Moisture control**: Resin powder should be **slightly damp** (not bone-dry) — residual water (~10–20%) prevents clumping and helps final hydration/conductivity. If powder is too dry, lightly mist with distilled water before mixing (very sparingly — excess water separates from cement).
- **Lumps/clumping**: Common if powder added too fast or too dry. Fix: add cement incrementally + vigorous stirring.
- **Too thick/difficult to brush**: Add 5–10% extra cement or thin with trace THF/cyclohexanone (from same brand if possible — not acetone, which may destabilize).
- **Too thin/runny**: Add more powder in small increments — aim for brushable but non-dripping.
- **Air bubbles**: Tap cup gently or use vacuum if available (rarely needed for small batches).
- **Batch size**: Start small (20–50 mL total) for tests — larger batches need more vigorous mixing (electric stirrer helps).
- **Variability by brand**: Different PVC cements have slightly different solvent blends/viscosity — test small batch first. Clear "regular body" works best; avoid filled or colored versions.

### Post-Mixing / Application Notes
- Brush or spatula-apply paste fully through fiberglass mesh (work both sides if needed for thick membranes).
- Let dry 30–60 minutes (still slightly tacky/moist internally) — do **not** let fully dry out.
- Soak in electrolyte (e.g., 1 M NaCl) to activate/swell resin.
- Never allow finished membrane to dry completely — store humid (sealed bag with damp sponge).

### Performance Expectations
- At 50:50 vol, expect low area resistance (~1–5 Ω·cm² after conditioning) and good permselectivity in simple tests (e.g., blocks methyl orange dye crossover while passing cations).
- Higher resin loading → better conductivity but more brittle/crumbly if over 70%.
- Lower → tougher but higher resistance.

This mixing is straightforward, forgiving for garage labs, and directly repeatable from the open-source videos/GitHub (e.g., ~1:1 vol is explicitly called out as the go-to). If you hit issues with a specific cement brand or resin type, note the details — small tweaks (e.g., ±10% ratio) often fix it. Happy building — this step turns cheap resin into something that rivals commercial CEMs!

# Fiberglass Mesh Reinforcement Details

**Fiberglass Mesh Reinforcement Details for DIY Ion-Exchange Membrane**

In Robert Karas' (@Rowow) open-source PVC-cement + resin membrane recipe, the fiberglass mesh serves as a **mechanical reinforcement** to prevent cracking, tearing, or excessive brittleness in the final composite membrane. The PVC-resin paste is brittle when cured (especially at high resin loadings for good conductivity), so embedding a woven mesh creates a flexible yet strong sheet that handles handling, swelling in electrolytes, and differential pressure in cells without delaminating or failing.

The mesh is **not** the primary ion conductor — it's inert and non-conductive — but it must be fully impregnated by the paste for integrity. The open weave allows the resin-PVC composite to penetrate and lock around the fibers, forming a true composite structure.

### Recommended Types & Specifications (from Original Video & Replications)
From the primary video (NightHawkInLight conversation) and follow-up builds (e.g., Rowow's own short demos, replications on YouTube/goldrefiningforum/FBRC):
- **Primary recommendation**: Standard **woven fiberglass mesh** — commonly described as "boat-hull reinforcement mesh" or similar lightweight fiberglass fabric.
  - **Mesh style**: Plain weave or leno weave (open grid).
  - **Mesh opening / hole size**: ~1–5 mm (coarse enough for easy paste penetration but fine enough for good support). Examples: 5x5 mm or 4x4 mm openings are common.
  - **Weight / areal density**: 80–160 g/m² (light to medium weight; heavier adds unnecessary thickness/stiffness).
  - **Thickness**: 0.2–0.5 mm (thin to avoid excessive bulk in the final membrane).
  - **Examples explicitly mentioned or shown**:
    - Boat-hull fiberglass reinforcement mesh (alkali-resistant E-glass, often sold for plaster/stucco or marine layup).
    - "Fiberglass strands, cut strands" (chopped strand mat alternatives, but woven preferred).
    - In some clips: "fiberglass mesh of some sort or woven sheet."
- **Alternative / budget option**: Fiberglass **window screen** or insect screen mesh (very fine weave, ~1–2 mm openings, lightweight ~30–60 g/m²).
  - Works well for thin membranes; easier to source (hardware stores like Bunnings in AU).
  - Drawback: Finer weave can make full impregnation slightly harder — brush paste aggressively both sides.
- **Avoid**:
  - Non-woven fiberglass mats (e.g., chopped strand mat without binder) — they mat down and don't provide isotropic strength.
  - Plastic meshes (nylon/polyester window screens) — PVC cement may not bond well or could dissolve.
  - Heavy roving or thick boat cloth (>300 g/m²) — makes membranes too stiff/thick, harder to cut/activate.
  - Alkali-sensitive untreated glass (for long-term electrolyte exposure; use AR — alkali-resistant — if possible, though short-term tests show standard E-glass holds up).

### Sourcing in Australia (Sydney/NSW Focus)
- **Hardware stores** (Bunnings Warehouse): Look in plastering/drywall section for "fibreglass mesh tape" or "render reinforcement mesh" (often 5x5 mm, 160 g/m² rolls, ~$10–20 for 50 m²).
- **Marine/boat supply**: Whitworths, JPW Marine, or online — "fibreglass cloth" or "boat building mesh" (lighter weights available).
- **Online**:
  - Amazon AU or eBay: Search "fiberglass mesh reinforcement" or "EIFS fiberglass mesh" (External Insulation Finishing System mesh — common for building, AR-glass, cheap in bulk).
  - AliExpress/Direct: "fiberglass mesh 5x5" or "plaster mesh" — rolls as low as $5–15 for large areas.
- **Cost**: <$5–10 per square meter; for A4-sized test membranes, a small scrap or 1 m roll suffices.

### Preparation & Cutting
- Cut to final desired membrane size + ~1–2 cm margin (trim after curing).
- Lay flat on non-stick surface (e.g., silicone sheet, polyethylene film, or wax paper — PVC cement won't bond permanently to these).
- Optional: Pre-wet mesh lightly with PVC cement (diluted if needed) for better initial adhesion.

### Impregnation/Application Technique
- Apply thick paste (50:50 resin powder:PVC cement) with brush or spatula.
- Work paste **fully through the weave** — push into holes so fibers are encapsulated on all sides.
- Brush both sides if mesh is thick; aim for ~0.5–1 mm total wet thickness (final dry ~0.3–0.8 mm).
- Multiple thin layers if needed for thicker/stronger membranes (let partially set between coats).
- Avoid air pockets — tap or roll gently.

### Why Fiberglass Specifically?
- Chemically inert in most electrolytes (acidic/neutral; test in strong base if needed).
- High tensile strength, low elongation → prevents cracking during swelling/deswelling.
- Good wet-out with PVC solvents → strong mechanical bond.
- Cheap, widely available, non-conductive (no shorting risk).

### Performance Notes from Replications
- Reinforced membranes are far more durable — unreinforced versions crack easily when flexed or swollen.
- In flow-battery/electrolysis tests: Handles >1 A/cm² currents, multiple cycles without delamination.
- For very thin/high-conductivity versions: Some builders skip mesh or use chopped strands mixed in, but mesh is standard for reliability.

This reinforcement step is straightforward and forgiving — the key is full impregnation and keeping everything slightly damp post-cure. If sourcing a specific type (e.g., exact mesh size/weight), grab a small roll for testing; most "plaster reinforcement" or "boat fiberglass mesh" variants work identically in this recipe. Let me know your exact setup or if you need alternatives (e.g., no-mesh versions)!

# Detailed paste mixing ratios

**Detailed Paste Mixing Ratios for DIY Ion-Exchange Membrane**

The paste is the critical composite in Robert Karas' (@Rowow) open-source recipe: finely ground cation-exchange resin powder (from water softener beads) bound in PVC cement (solvent-based, e.g., clear regular-body Oatey or equivalent). The ratio directly trades off **ion conductivity** (higher resin loading → more functional groups, lower resistance) against **mechanical integrity** (higher PVC → better cohesion, less brittleness/cracking upon swelling/drying cycles). Too much resin leads to crumbly or porous films; too little yields high-resistance, low-permeability membranes.

### Primary / Recommended Ratio (Most Reproducible & Commonly Used)
- **50:50 by volume** resin powder : PVC cement  
  - This is the "standard" or "demonstration" ratio explicitly stated and demonstrated in Rowow's videos (e.g., "This powder is now mixed with a 50% volutric ratio of PVC cement" and "same volume of the PVC cement to the same volume of resin").
  - Equivalent shorthand: **1:1 vol** or **50/50 vol**.
  - Why it works best for most users: Balances conductivity (~1–5 Ω·cm² area resistance after activation in replications) with durability (handles swelling in electrolytes without delaminating from fiberglass).
  - Visual consistency: Thick, spreadable paste like heavy peanut butter or thick paint — holds shape when scooped but brushes smoothly through mesh weave.

### Weight-Based Equivalent for Precision / Repeatability
Resin powder (slightly damp, post-grinding) has bulk density ~0.7–0.8 g/cm³; PVC cement ~1.0–1.1 g/cm³ (solvent-heavy).
- Approximate **1:1.2 to 1:1.5 by mass** (resin powder : PVC cement) to match 50:50 vol.
  - Example for small batch: 40 g resin powder + 50–60 g PVC cement.
  - Use a digital scale for batches >20 mL total — volumetric scoops work for tests but vary with powder packing/moisture.

### Range for Optimization / Experimentation
From Rowow's own statements and replications:
- **10–70% resin powder by volume** (i.e., 10:90 to 70:30 resin:PVC cement vol).
  - Lower end (10–30% resin): Tougher, more flexible membranes; higher resistance; good for mechanical stress or thin films.
  - Higher end (60–70% resin): Excellent conductivity/permselectivity; more brittle; risk of cracking if not reinforced well or if dried fully.
  - "Aim for around 50%" is the sweet spot for general-purpose (e.g., flow batteries, electrolysis demos).
- Adjustments reported in community builds:
  - 40:60 resin:PVC → Slightly tougher for handling.
  - 60:40 resin:PVC → Lower resistance in high-current tests but needs careful curing/humidity control.

### Mixing Protocol Recap with Ratio Focus
1. Measure resin powder first (vol or mass) into disposable cup.
2. Add PVC cement in the chosen ratio (start with 50:50 vol for baseline).
3. Stir vigorously 1–2 min until uniform — no dry pockets.
   - If too thick (clumpy, won't spread): Add cement in 5–10% increments.
   - If too thin (runny, drips off brush): Add powder in small amounts.
4. Optional rest 1–2 min to degas/minor solvent penetration.
5. Apply immediately to mesh (within 5–15 min) — paste thickens as solvents evaporate.

### Influencing Factors & Fine-Tuning Tips
- **Resin moisture**: Slightly damp powder (~10–20% residual) mixes better and aids final hydration — dry powder clumps more, may need +5–10% cement.
- **PVC cement brand/viscosity**: Clear "regular" or "medium body" best; "heavy duty" or filled versions may need dilution (trace THF from same brand, not acetone — avoid destabilizing).
- **Batch size**: Small (20–50 mL total) for tests; larger needs electric stirrer for uniformity.
- **Testing tweaks**:
  - Measure resistance post-activation (soak in 1 M NaCl, apply voltage to condition).
  - Check permselectivity (e.g., methyl orange vs. methylene blue dye test — low crossover = good).
  - If brittle/cracks on flex: Drop to 40–45% resin.
  - If high resistance/low current: Increase to 55–60% resin.

This range (centered on 50:50 vol) comes directly from Rowow's demos and open-source intent — start there, characterize your membrane (simple H-cell electrolysis test), then iterate ±10% based on your application (e.g., high-conductivity for batteries vs. durability for long-term electrolysis). The recipe is forgiving; most failures stem from full drying or poor impregnation rather than exact ratio. If you share your resin brand, cement type, or test results, I can suggest precise tweaks! - **Cation-exchange resin beads** — Standard sodium-form water softener resin (e.g., Purolite C100E or generic from Amazon/hardware stores; avoid mixed-bed or anion resins).
- **Clear PVC cement** — Regular/medium body, clear (e.g., Oatey Clear PVC Cement or equivalent; **not** purple primer, not CPVC, not heavy-duty filled versions).
- **Reinforcement** (optional but strongly recommended for durability): Woven fiberglass mesh (plaster/render reinforcement or boat-hull grade, ~4–5 mm openings, 80–160 g/m²).
- **Non-stick surface** for casting: Polypropylene sheet, silicone mat, polyethylene film, or wax paper (PVC cement releases easily once dry).
- **Tools**: High-speed spice/grain blender or grinder, disposable plastic cup, brush/spatula/stick for mixing & applying, digital scale (optional for precision), ventilated workspace.

### Exact Step-by-Step Procedure
1. **Grind the Resin to Super-Fine Powder**  
   - Take moist/damp resin beads (as supplied; do **not** fully dry them — residual moisture prevents clumping and preserves capacity).  
   - Grind in high-speed electric spice/grain/herb grinder (150–300 W) in small batches (50–100 g).  
     - Pulse in short bursts (5–10 s) to avoid overheating.  
     - Do 3–5 cycles of 20–30 s each, shaking/stirring between.  
     - Total: 2–5 minutes until powder is silky, flour/talc-like (<50–100 µm; no visible grit when rubbed).  
   - Optional upgrade: Pre-grind in ball mill (4–12 h with ceramic/steel media) for uniformity, then finish in grinder.  
   - Keep powder slightly moist — store immediately in airtight container with humidity source if not using right away.

2. **Mix Paste (50:50 Volume Ratio — The Standard & Most Reliable)**  
   - Measure equal volumes of resin powder and clear PVC cement (e.g., 50 mL powder + 50 mL cement; or weigh ~40 g powder + 50–60 g cement for ~1:1.3 mass ratio).  
   - Add powder to disposable cup first.  
   - Slowly add PVC cement while stirring vigorously with stick/spatula (1–2 minutes).  
   - Result: Thick, uniform paste like heavy peanut butter or thick paint — fully wets powder, no dry pockets, spreadable but holds shape.  
     - Too thick/clumpy → add cement 5–10% at a time.  
     - Too runny → add powder incrementally.  
   - Let rest 1–2 min (degas/minor solvent penetration), remix briefly.  
   - Use within 5–15 min (paste thickens as solvents evaporate).

3. **Apply to Reinforcement / Cast**  
   - **With fiberglass mesh** (recommended for strong, flexible membranes):  
     - Cut mesh to size + margin.  
     - Lay flat on non-stick surface.  
     - Brush/spatula thick paste fully through weave (both sides if needed) — ensure complete impregnation, no air pockets.  
     - Aim for ~0.5–1 mm wet thickness.  
   - **Without mesh** (simpler but more brittle): Pour/spread paste directly onto non-stick sheet (polypropylene recommended) to desired thickness/area.  
   - Work in well-ventilated area (fumes from THF/cyclohexanone in cement).

4. **Dry / Cure**  
   - Air-dry at room temperature for ~30–60 minutes (or until firm/tacky but **not fully dry** inside).  
   - Critical: **Do not let it dry out completely** — retain internal moisture for ion conductivity. Membrane should feel slightly damp/tacky when peeled.  
   - Peel gently from non-stick surface once set (it releases easily).

5. **Trim & Store**  
   - Trim to final size.  
   - Immediately store in sealed container with humidity (e.g., small amount of water or damp paper towel in bottom compartment) — **never allow full drying**, or performance drops dramatically.

6. **Activation / Conditioning (Required for Use)**  
   - Soak in target electrolyte (e.g., 1 M NaCl, KCl, or your battery/electrolysis solution) for several hours to days.  
   - Apply low voltage (1–5 V) across membrane in simple cell to force ion migration and reduce resistance (condition for 30–60 min or until current stabilizes).  
   - Swap to desired ionic form if needed (e.g., acid soak for H⁺ form).

### Quick Validation Tests
- **Dye test** (permselectivity): Place membrane in H-cell with methylene blue (cationic) + methyl orange (anionic) dyes + baking soda (raises pH) — good membrane blocks methyl orange crossover while allowing cations.  
- **Electrolysis test**: Simple CuSO₄ | membrane | NaCl cell with carbon electrodes — expect directional ion flow, low crossover.  
- **Resistance check**: Measure in 1 M NaCl after conditioning (aim <5–10 Ω·cm² for good performance).

### Key Rules for Success
- Keep everything **slightly moist** throughout (resin powder, finished membrane).  
- Use **clear regular PVC cement** — brand variations minor but test small batch.  
- Ventilate — solvent fumes are strong.  
- Start with 50:50 vol; tweak to 40–60% resin based on your needs (higher resin = better conductivity, lower = tougher).  
- Reinforce with fiberglass for real-world use (unreinforced cracks easily when swollen).

This is the **exact core process** as open-sourced by Rowow — 3 main steps (grind, mix, apply/dry) plus activation. Full details/patent/build files are in the GitHub repo (https://github.com/Rowow1/Open-sourced-off-the-shelf-ion-exchange-membrane). Replicate safely in a garage/lab setup — it's deliberately simple and effective for flow batteries, electrolysis, metal recovery, etc. If you run a test batch and hit issues (e.g., specific resin brand), share observations for tweaks!

# chemials

**Chemicals to Power a Battery Using This DIY Ion-Exchange Membrane**

Robert Karas (@Rowow) developed this low-cost cation-exchange membrane (CEM) primarily for **redox flow batteries** (RFBs), as mentioned in the main NightHawkInLight interview and his videos. It separates the positive and negative electrolytes while allowing cations (e.g., H⁺, Na⁺, K⁺, or metal ions) to pass, preventing crossover of active species.

The membrane is versatile and works with many aqueous chemistries, but Rowow has demonstrated or referenced **simple, cheap, and safe options** suitable for DIY/garage builds. Here's a breakdown of practical chemicals/electrolytes, based on his demos, mentions, and common low-cost RFB pairings that match the membrane's capabilities (cation-selective, good in acidic/neutral pH, hardware-store compatible):

### Recommended Starter Chemistry: Iron-Based (Fe²⁺/Fe³⁺ Redox Couple)
This is the most straightforward and frequently referenced for DIY setups with similar membranes — **all-iron aqueous redox flow battery** style.
- **Positive electrolyte (catholyte, oxidized form)**: FeCl₃ (ferric chloride) solution — yellow/orange when charged.
- **Negative electrolyte (anolyte, reduced form)**: FeCl₂ (ferrous chloride) solution — pale green when discharged.
- **Supporting electrolyte / salt**: Add NaCl (table salt), KCl, NH₄Cl (ammonium chloride), or HCl for conductivity and pH control (typically 1–3 M total salt).
- **Typical concentrations** (from similar all-iron systems and DIY adaptations):
  - 0.5–1.5 M FeCl₂ / FeCl₃ (start low, e.g., 0.5 M to avoid precipitation).
  - Dissolve in water + supporting salt (e.g., 2–3 M NH₄Cl or NaCl for better solubility/stability).
- **Voltage & energy**: ~1.0–1.2 V open-circuit per cell; practical for low-power demos (e.g., lighting LEDs or small loads).
- **Why this works well here**:
  - Cheap & available (FeCl₃ from etching suppliers/hardware, FeCl₂ from chemical shops or made by reducing FeCl₃ with iron filings).
  - Aqueous, non-flammable, relatively safe (handle with gloves — FeCl₃ is corrosive).
  - Rowow's membrane excels in chloride-based systems (low crossover in tests).
  - In one demo, he showed a small stationary iron-chloride setup reaching "four plus volts" and "over 1 amp hour" without flow — but full performance needs circulation.
- **Preparation tips**:
  - Start with FeCl₂ solution on both sides (symmetric), charge to convert one side to FeCl₃.
  - pH ~1–3 (add dilute HCl if needed to prevent Fe³⁺ hydrolysis/precipitation).

### Other Accessible Chemistries Mentioned or Compatible
- **Copper-based** (Cu²⁺/Cu⁺ or Cu²⁺ plating — hybrid flow): CuSO₄ + H₂SO₄ (copper sulfate + sulfuric acid) — common in DIY electrolysis demos.
- **Vanadium** (classic VRFB): VOSO₄ (vanadyl sulfate) in H₂SO₄ — higher performance (~1.4 V) but expensive/toxic vanadium; not ideal for cheap DIY.
- **Zinc-bromine** or **zinc-iron**: ZnBr₂ or ZnCl₂ — but bromine is hazardous.
- **Organic/acid-based**: Possible with quinones or other organics, but Rowow focuses on inorganic for simplicity.

**Avoid** strong oxidizers (e.g., high-concentration nitric/perchloric) or extreme pH that degrade PVC binder long-term.

### Do You Need to Pump the Liquid? (Flow Requirement)

**Short answer: No for basic proof-of-concept / small stationary batteries, but yes for high performance, long runtime, or real energy storage.**

- **No pumping needed (static / non-flow mode)**:
  - Rowow explicitly demonstrated a small, **non-flowing** iron-chloride battery that stored >1 Ah and produced voltage without any pumps — electrolytes stay in small chambers on each side of the membrane.
  - Great for testing membrane function, simple demos, or very low-power applications (e.g., trickle charging, sensors).
  - Limitation: Low power density (limited by diffusion to electrodes); capacity limited by chamber volume; uneven concentration builds up over time.

- **Pumping required for true redox flow battery advantages**:
  - To achieve high power, deep discharge, long cycle life, and scalability → **pump the electrolytes** continuously or intermittently from external tanks through the cell.
  - Keeps fresh reactant at electrodes → higher current (amps vs. milliamps), better efficiency.
  - Decouples energy (tank size) from power (cell area) — classic RFB benefit.
  - In Rowow's discussions: "pump pumping the liquids uh between the electro and membrane really simple" — he notes flow is used in proper RFBs for efficiency.
  - Simple DIY pump setup: Small 12V peristaltic or diaphragm pump (~$10–30 online), tubing, two bottles/tanks as reservoirs. Flow rate: 10–100 mL/min per cell (adjustable).
  - Without flow: Expect lower output and faster "dead spots" near electrodes.

**Recommendation for Steve in Sydney**:
- Start static/no-flow: Mix 0.5 M FeCl₂ + 2 M NaCl (or NH₄Cl) on both sides, charge one side to Fe³⁺, test voltage/current with carbon electrodes.
- Add cheap pumps later for better performance (Bunnings or Jaycar for tubing/pumps).
- Safety: Work in ventilated area, use gloves/goggles — FeCl₃ stains and corrodes; neutralize spills with baking soda.

This iron setup is the easiest/cheapest to replicate from Rowow's hints — low risk, hardware-store friendly. If you build it, share your results for tweaks!

The original **iron-chloride (FeCl₂/FeCl₃)** setup I suggested is cheap and accessible in Sydney (FeCl₃ from pool/etching suppliers like Bunnings or online, FeCl₂ derivable), but it's moderately **corrosive** (acidic, stains, irritates skin/metal). Rowow's DIY cation-exchange membrane (PVC + resin) handles chloride systems well, but for **safer, less corrosive** options (near-neutral pH, minimal fumes/risk, household-friendly), here are the most practical alternatives based on DIY-friendly, low-cost aqueous chemistries.    - **Electrolytes**:
     - Anolyte (negative side): ZnCl₂ (zinc chloride) + supporting salt like NH₄Cl (ammonium chloride) or NaCl.
     - Catholyte (positive side): KI (potassium iodide) or NaI + same supporting salt.
     - Typical mix: 1–2 M ZnCl₂ + 1–2 M KI in water, with NH₄Cl for conductivity (~1–3 M total salts).

   - **Performance**: ~1.2–1.4 V cell voltage, good for small DIY cells. During charge, zinc plates on one electrode (reversible), iodine forms on the other.
   - **Availability in Sydney**:
     - ZnCl₂: Pool shops (flux for galvanizing), eBay/chemical suppliers (~$20–50/kg).
     - KI: Iodine supplements or lab suppliers (eBay ~$30–60/500g), or derive from tincture + reduction.

2. **All-Iron Sulfate (FeSO₄-based) Aqueous Flow Battery** — Milder version of iron
   - **Electrolytes**: FeSO₄ (ferrous sulfate) in water + supporting salt (e.g., Na₂SO₄ or (NH₄)₂SO₄).
   - **Why safer**: Much less corrosive than chloride (pH ~3–5, no HCl fumes), FeSO₄ is garden fertilizer ("iron sulphate" at Bunnings ~$10–20/kg).
   - **Performance**: Lower voltage (~1 V), but cheap and stable. Some versions use ionic liquids/additives for higher concentration.
   - **Availability**: Super easy — pool/fertilizer section.
   - **Cost**: Extremely low (~$50/kWh estimated in papers).
   - **Note**: May need additives for stability; less documented in pure DIY but compatible with your CEM.

3. **Organic-Based (Aqueous Organic Redox Flow Battery — AORFB) Variants** — Future-proof, truly non-corrosive
   - **Examples**:
     - Methyl viologen (MV, paraquat derivative — anolyte) + 4-HO-TEMPO (catholyte) in NaCl water solution.
     - Quinones/anthraquinones (e.g., DHAQ or AQDS) paired with ferrocyanide or TEMPO.
     - Phenazines or lignin-derived organics (emerging, very cheap/abundant).
   - **Why ideal for safety**: Neutral pH, water-like viscosity, non-toxic/low-toxicity organics, no metals/acids. Cell voltage ~1.2–1.25 V.
   - **Availability**: Harder for true DIY — MV/TEMPO from chemical suppliers (not hardware stores), but some use food-grade dyes or simple organics. NaCl is table salt.
   - **Cost**: Potentially <$100/kWh; organics scalable/cheap in bulk.
   - **Drawback**: Less mature for garage builds; sourcing redox molecules may need online chem suppliers. Your membrane works well here (cation exchange suits many).

# Zinc-Iodide DIY Build Guide

	**Zinc-Iodide (Zn/I or Zn-I₂) Redox Flow Battery DIY Build Guide**

The zinc-iodide redox flow battery is an excellent next step from your iron-chloride setup using Robert Karas' (@Rowow) DIY cation-exchange membrane. ### Electrolyte Recipe (from FBRC & Common DIY Sources)
Symmetric start (same mix both sides), then charge to separate:
- **Main salts**:
  - ZnCl₂ (zinc chloride): 1–2 M (start 1 M for safety/solubility).
  - KI (potassium iodide): 1–2 M (or higher for capacity; KI helps form soluble I₃⁻).
  - Supporting salt: NH₄Cl (ammonium chloride) or NaCl: 1–3 M for conductivity & stability.
- **Example practical mix** (small batch, ~100–200 mL per side):
  - Dissolve 13.6 g ZnCl₂ + 16.6 g KI + 10.7 g NH₄Cl in ~100 mL distilled water (adjust for concentration).
  - Stir until clear; pH ~neutral-mildly acidic.
  - Source in Sydney: ZnCl₂ (pool flux/etchant, Bunnings/eBay ~$20–50/kg), KI (supplements/chem suppliers eBay ~$30–60/500g), NH₄Cl (fertilizer/hardware ~$10/kg).

1. **Simplest Static/H-Cell Test (No Pump, Quick Proof-of-Concept)**

   - Connect with tubing or direct contact.

   - Discharge to a small load (LED/resistor).
   - Similar to non-flow iron demos; expect low power (~mA) but proves membrane/chemistry.

2. **DIY Flow Setup (Recommended for Real Performance)**
   - Follow FBRC open-source guide (https://fbrc.codeberg.page/rfb-dev-kit/): Detailed steps for cell assembly, jig, electronics, electrolyte prep, testing.
   - **Core components** (adapt to your budget):
     - Cell: 3D-print or laser-cut flow frames (or use acrylic/PVC sheets with channels).

     - Separator: Your DIY CEM (ideal) or matte photo paper (cheap alternative in their design).
     - Pumps: Peristaltic (12V, ~$10–30 each from Jaycar/eBay) — flow 10–100 mL/min.
     - Reservoirs: Plastic bottles/jars.
     - Control: Arduino for pump timing; optional open-source potentiostat for cycling.
   - Assembly flow: Attach tubing → stack cell (frame + electrode + separator + electrode + frame) → clamp/jig → connect pumps/reservoirs → fill electrolyte → leak test with water → charge/discharge.

   - Gravity/non-rechargeable versions: Zinc + iodine solution — powerful short bursts but not rechargeable.

# Simple, Household Electrolytes

**Other Super-Simple, Household Electrolytes for DIY Batteries (Salt, Vinegar, HCl, Aluminum, Air/O₂)**

You're asking about ultra-cheap, easy-to-get household items like **table salt (NaCl)**, **vinegar (acetic acid)**, **HCl** (from drain cleaners or pool acid), **aluminum** (foil/scrap), and **air/oxygen** as the oxidant. These work great for **metal-air batteries** (not true reversible redox flow batteries like zinc-iodide), but they're extremely accessible, safe-ish (with care), low-corrosive in mild versions, and perfect for garage experiments in Sydney. They use atmospheric oxygen (O₂ from air) at the cathode, so no need for expensive catholytes — just expose one side to air.

These are **primary (non-rechargeable)** batteries — they consume the metal "fuel" (aluminum or zinc) over time, producing electricity until the metal is used up. You "refuel" by adding fresh metal foil/scrap. They're not flow batteries (no pumping needed for basic versions), but some DIYers add simple flow for better performance.

Your Rowow DIY cation-exchange membrane can separate compartments if you want to experiment with asymmetric setups or prevent mixing, but many builds are simple single-cell without it.

### 1. Aluminum-Air Battery (Top Pick for Your List — High Voltage, Cheap "Fuel")
- **Why it fits**: Aluminum foil/scrap is everywhere (kitchen foil, cans), air provides free O₂, electrolytes are household. Voltage ~1.2–1.6 V per cell (higher than zinc-iodide in some builds). Power enough for LEDs, small motors, or phone charging demos.
- **Electrolyte options** (from easiest/safest to stronger):
  - **Salt water** (table salt + water): Safest, least corrosive. Saturated solution (~300 g NaCl per liter water). Add a pinch of baking soda (NaHCO₃) or washing soda (Na₂CO₃) to boost OH⁻ for better performance.
  - **Salt + vinegar**: Mild acid helps remove aluminum oxide layer (passivation). Mix saturated salt water + 5–10% vinegar. Less corrosive than pure HCl, common in kid-safe science projects.
  - **Dilute HCl**: Stronger (from hardware drain cleaner like "muriatic acid" or pool pH down — dilute to 5–10%). Boosts reaction rate hugely but more corrosive/fumes — use gloves, ventilation, outdoors. Not recommended for beginners.
- **Build basics**:
  - **Anode**: Aluminum foil ball, sheet, or scrap (clean with sandpaper to remove oxide).
  - **Cathode**: Activated charcoal (from fish tank filter, BBQ briquettes crushed, or carbon felt) + air exposure (fan helps O₂ supply).
  - **Separator/electrolyte holder**: Paper towel, cloth, or your DIY membrane soaked in electrolyte.
  - **Simple setup**: Wrap aluminum in paper towel soaked in electrolyte, press against carbon/air cathode (e.g., charcoal in mesh bag). Connect wires.
  - **Flow version**: Pump electrolyte through cell (peristaltic pump) to refresh O₂/ remove Al(OH)₃ gel byproduct — some YouTube builds show "flow" to stop corrosion when idle.
- **Performance**: 1–1.5 V, 10–100 mA easy. Stack cells for more voltage (e.g., 5–10 V). Byproduct is aluminum hydroxide (gel, harmless).
- **Safety/availability in Sydney**: Bunnings for salt, vinegar, charcoal; foil from supermarket. HCl from pool shops (dilute carefully). Low toxicity, but avoid skin contact with strong mixes.

### 2. Zinc-Air or Aluminum-Zinc Variants (Even Safer Alternatives)
- Similar to aluminum-air but with zinc (from old batteries, galvanized nails, or pure zinc sheet).
- Electrolyte: Salt water or salt + vinegar (vinegar prevents zinc corrosion better).
- Often combined: Aluminum anode + zinc for hybrid power.
- Household coin battery style: Stack pennies (copper), aluminum foil, vinegar-soaked paper — but low power.

### 3. Iron-Air (Rust Battery — Ultra-Cheap Refuelable)
- **Anode**: Iron/steel scrap (rebar, nails).
- **Electrolyte**: Salt water + vinegar (forms sodium acetate, mild).
- **Cathode**: Carbon + air.
- Refuel by replacing rusty iron with fresh scrap. Very low cost, but lower voltage (~0.8–1 V).

# Magnesium-air battery variants

# Zinc-Air Batteries

**Zinc-Air Batteries: DIY-Friendly Overview & Build Guide**

Zinc-air (Zn-air) batteries are metal-air systems where zinc metal oxidizes at the anode, and oxygen from the air reduces at the cathode. They're popular in commercial hearing aids due to high energy density (~300–400 Wh/kg practical, higher than many batteries), low cost, and safety (aqueous electrolyte, non-flammable). Theoretical voltage is ~1.65 V, but DIY/homemade versions often hit 1.2–1.4 V open-circuit.

Compared to the metal-air variants we've discussed:
- **Vs. Aluminum-Air**: Zn-air is generally safer/less corrosive (less aggressive self-discharge and H₂ evolution), easier to control, and more stable in alkaline electrolytes. Al-air often gives higher current but more passivation issues and needs activation.
- **Vs. Magnesium-Air**: Mg-air can reach higher voltage (~1.8–2.0 V) but corrodes faster (more H₂ gas, shorter runtime). Zn-air is more forgiving for beginners.
- Overall: Zn-air strikes the best balance for DIY — reliable voltage, moderate corrosiveness (especially in mild setups), and abundant materials.

These are **primary** batteries (non-rechargeable in simple DIY form) — "refuel" by replacing zinc. Rechargeable versions exist in labs/startups but require bifunctional catalysts, zincate suppression, and are hard to DIY reliably at home.

### Electrolyte Options (Household-Friendly to Stronger)
Commercial Zn-air uses strong alkaline (30–40% KOH), but DIY can use milder for safety.

1. **Salt Water (Mildest, Safest Household Version)**  
   - Saturated NaCl (table salt) in water (~1 level tbsp per cup water).  
   - Voltage: ~0.75–1.0 V (lower but very safe, no caustic fumes).  
   - From sources like Science Buddies project — great for kids/science fair.

2. **Salt Water + Additives**  
   - Add hydrogen peroxide (H₂O₂, from pharmacy) for higher voltage (~1–1.2 V boost via extra oxidant).  
   - Or vinegar (acetic acid) trace for activation.

3. **Alkaline (Best Performance, Standard DIY)**  
   - Sodium hydroxide (NaOH, "caustic soda" from Bunnings drain cleaner) — dissolve 50 g in 150 mL water (exothermic — add slowly, cool before use).  
   - Or potassium hydroxide (KOH flakes, similar sourcing).  
   - This is the classic from NurdRage/Instructables — ~1.2–1.4 V, good current.  
   - Corrosive — gloves, goggles, ventilation essential.

### Simple DIY Build Protocol (NurdRage-Style Classic, Most Reproducible)
From NurdRage's video/Instructables (widely replicated):

**Materials** (easy in Sydney):
- **Anode**: Zinc sheet/foil (strip from old zinc-carbon batteries, galvanized nails stripped, or pure zinc sheet/ribbon from eBay ~$10–20).  
- **Cathode**: Steel wool (from Bunnings) or activated charcoal/carbon felt (fish tank filter, crushed BBQ briquettes).  
- **Separator**: Paper towel, cloth, or filter paper (soaked in electrolyte).  
- **Electrolyte**: As above (start with NaOH for best results).  
- **Container**: Plastic jar/cup.  
- **Wires**: Alligator clips or copper wire.  
- Optional: Your Rowow CEM as separator for better isolation.

**Steps**:
1. Prepare electrolyte: Dissolve NaOH (50 g in 150 mL water) slowly — it heats up. Cool to room temp. (Or use salt water for safer start.)  
2. Anode: Attach wire to zinc sheet/strip (sand if oxidized).  
3. Cathode: Attach wire to steel wool bundle. Wrap steel wool in paper towel (separator) for air exposure.  
4. Assemble: Wrap zinc around the wrapped steel wool (like a roll). Place in container so steel wool top is exposed to air.  
5. Add electrolyte: Pour in enough to soak separator but keep cathode dry/exposed (oxygen needs air access).  
6. Test: Connect multimeter — expect ~1.2–1.4 V open-circuit. Current rises with surface area/air flow (fan helps). Power small LED or buzzer.  
7. Refuel: Replace spent zinc (forms white ZnO/Zn(OH)₂).

**Tips & Performance**:
- Voltage: 1.2–1.4 V typical; stack cells for higher (e.g., 3–6 V).  
- Current: 10–100 mA easy; higher with larger area/fresh air.  
- Runtime: Hours to days depending on zinc amount/current draw.  
- Issues: Cathode flooding (keep dry side open); zinc corrosion in alkaline (white gel forms — normal).  
- Safer variant: Use salt water — lower voltage but no caustic risk.

### Other Variants
- **Saltwater Zinc-Air** (Science Buddies): Zinc strip + copper cathode in saltwater — simple demo, ~0.75 V.  
- **High-Voltage/No-Separator** (some YouTube): Dual electrolytes (acid + alkaline) for >2 V, but more complex/corrosive.  
- **Zinc-Ion Hybrids** (Reddit/DIYSolarForum): Not true air, but Zn-MnO₂ or Zn-ion with slurry — rechargeable-ish, scalable.

No widespread open-source kits exist for true Zn-air (commercial hearing aid tech is proprietary), but the NurdRage/Instructables build is the go-to for garage replication.

This is easier/safer than Mg-air (less gas) and more stable than Al-air for beginners. Start with the alkaline version if you're comfortable with NaOH (common in drain cleaners), or salt water for zero-risk testing. **Zinc-Ion Battery DIY Build Guide (Rechargeable Aqueous Version)**

Zinc-ion batteries (ZIBs or aqueous zinc-ion batteries, AZIBs) are an emerging, safe, and low-cost rechargeable chemistry using zinc metal anodes, mild aqueous electrolytes (usually ZnSO₄-based), and various cathode materials. They avoid lithium's flammability/toxicity and are promising for grid/storage applications. True **rechargeable zinc-ion** cells are more advanced than simple zinc-air or zinc-carbon primaries — they rely on reversible Zn²⁺ intercalation/deintercalation at the cathode and zinc plating/stripping at the anode.

DIY/homemade versions exist in the garage/hobbyist space (e.g., from YouTube channels like SMP/MicroPhotography, Cayrex, and DIY Solar Forum threads), often achieving 0.5–2 Ah in small cells with ~100–200 Wh/kg on active materials. These are **not** as efficient as commercial ones but are fully reproducible at home with cheap materials. They use your Rowow DIY cation-exchange membrane (or filter paper/glass fiber) as a separator to reduce dendrite/short risks.

**Important Warnings**  
- Rechargeable zinc batteries can form dendrites (sharp zinc growths) → shorts/fire risk if overcharged or poorly designed.  
- Use low currents initially; monitor voltage/temperature.  
- Electrolyte is mildly acidic — gloves/goggles/ventilation.  
- Not for high-power use without testing; start small (coin-cell or pouch size).

### Key Components & Materials (Easy Sourcing in Sydney)
- **Anode**: Zinc foil/sheet/ribbon (99.9% pure preferred; eBay/Amazon AU ~$10–30 for 100g; or strip from old zinc-carbon batteries/hardware galvanized items — sand clean).  
- **Cathode**: Activated carbon (coconut shell from water filters, BBQ briquettes crushed, or fish tank carbon — cheapest/high-surface); or MnO₂ (from old alkaline batteries — disassemble carefully); or V₂O₅/Vanadium oxide if sourced (advanced). Many DIY use **activated carbon** for supercapacitor-like hybrid behavior (high capacity, fast charge).  
- **Electrolyte**: 1–3 M ZnSO₄ (zinc sulfate heptahydrate — pool/fertilizer shops, eBay ~$20/kg) in distilled water. Optional: Add 0.1–0.5 M MnSO₄ for Mn-based cathodes or trace additives (e.g., Na₂SO₄) for stability. Mild pH ~4–5, safe-ish.  
- **Separator**: Your DIY PVC-resin ion-exchange membrane (excellent for cation selectivity, blocks large anions); or glass fiber filter paper, lab filter paper, or even thick paper towel (soaked).  
- **Current collectors**: Carbon cloth/felt (eBay), stainless steel mesh, or copper foil (tape to avoid direct Zn contact).  
- **Housing**: Plastic pouch (zip-lock modified), 3D-printed case, or small plastic container/jar.  
- **Tools**: Multimeter, power supply/charger (for constant current), alligator clips, scale.

### Simple Rechargeable Zinc-Ion Build Protocol (Based on Hobbyist Replications)
This follows common DIY approaches (e.g., SMP's 1 Ah hybrid, DIY Solar Forum ~190 Wh/kg claims, Cayrex variants) — symmetric carbon or carbon/MnO₂ cathode.

1. **Prepare Electrodes**  
   - Anode: Cut zinc foil to size (e.g., 5×5 cm for small cell). Attach wire/current collector. Sand lightly.  
   - Cathode: Mix activated carbon (80%) + conductive additive (e.g., graphite powder 10%) + binder (e.g., PVA glue or starch 10%). Press/slurry onto carbon cloth or foil (dry thoroughly). For MnO₂ version: Crush MnO₂ from alkaline battery + carbon + binder. Aim for high surface area.

2. **Electrolyte**  
   - Dissolve 100–300 g ZnSO₄·7H₂O per liter distilled water (start 2 M for good conductivity). Stir until clear. Optional: Add 0.1 M MnSO₄ if using Mn cathode.

3. **Assemble Cell (Swagelok/Coin or Pouch Style)**  
   - Stack: Zinc anode | separator (your CEM or filter paper soaked in electrolyte) | cathode.  
   - Press together in plastic housing (clamp or zip pouch sealed with hot glue/tape).  
   - Add electrolyte to soak separator/electrodes (avoid excess to prevent leakage).  
   - Seal (leave small vent if gas evolves initially).

4. **Formation/Activation**  
   - Charge slowly at low current (e.g., 1–5 mA for small cell) to 1.8–2.0 V (Zn stripping/plating).  
   - Discharge to ~0.8–1.0 V. Cycle 5–10 times at low rate to form stable SEI/passivation.  
   - Typical operating window: 0.8–1.8 V to avoid H₂ evolution or dendrite growth.

5. **Cycling**  
   - Use constant current charger (e.g., hobby LiPo charger in NiMH mode or Arduino setup).  
   - Expect 0.5–1.5 V average discharge; capacity 100–500 mAh/g cathode in good builds.  
   - Cycle life: 100–500+ cycles possible if dendrites controlled (low current, additives help).

### Expected Performance & Variants
- **Voltage**: 1.0–1.5 V average (higher than zinc-air primaries).  
- **Capacity**: 0.5–2 Ah in small DIY cells (e.g., SMP's 1 Ah hybrid with carbon).  
- **Energy density**: Up to ~150–200 Wh/kg on active materials in optimized garage builds.  
- **Variants**:  
  - **Carbon hybrid** (supercap-like): Fast charge, high power, lower energy.  
  - **MnO₂ cathode**: Higher capacity but Mn dissolution over cycles.  
  - **Dual electrolyte** (advanced, e.g., Cayrex): Acid on cathode side + alkaline on anode for >2 V — but more complex/corrosive.  
  - **Zinc-chlorine** or **zinc-bromine**: Flow versions possible but hazardous gases.

### Tips & Troubleshooting
- **Dendrites**: Limit charge current (<10 mA/cm²), add surfactants (trace dish soap), or use pulsed charging.  
- **Low capacity**: Increase cathode surface area, use purer Zn, condition longer.  
- **Shorts**: Check separator integrity; use thicker or your CEM.  
- **Sourcing**: ZnSO₄ from pool shops (as algaecide/fertilizer); carbon from aquarium filters; zinc from eBay.

### Top Cheap & Safe Chemicals/Electrolytes Ranked by Current/Power Potential
From DIY replications (YouTube garage builds, hobbyist forums, science projects) and research on metal-air/zinc systems, here's what gives the **highest current** while staying cheap/safe(ish). Focus on **metal-air** types (zinc-air, aluminum-air) — they use free air O₂, so high theoretical power if optimized (large surface area, good air flow, activated cathode).

1. **Potassium Hydroxide (KOH) — Alkaline Electrolyte (Highest Current Winner)**
   - **Why highest current**: Strong alkaline electrolytes like 20–45% KOH (or 4–8 M) give the best conductivity (~0.6 S/cm), fast ORR (oxygen reduction) kinetics, and low resistance. DIY tests show 100–500+ mA/cm² peaks in zinc-air or Al-air with good cathode (activated carbon + MnO₂ catalyst).
     - Zinc-air: Often 100–300 mA continuous, up to 1–2 A bursts in small cells.
     - Al-air: Even higher bursts (500 mA–1 A+ per coin-sized cell in videos) due to higher anode activity.
   - **Cheap & sourcing in Sydney**: "Caustic soda" or drain cleaner (pure KOH flakes/pellets from Bunnings/hardware ~$10–20/kg). Dissolve 200–400 g in 1 L water (exothermic — add slowly, cool, use gloves/goggles/ventilation).
   - **Safety**: Corrosive (burns skin/eyes), slippery soap-like on skin, produces H₂ gas bubbles (ventilate). Not "food safe" but safer than acids for EV (no chlorine gas risk). Use plastic containers.
   - **Best for DIY EV**: Highest power for acceleration. Use with zinc foil/plate or aluminum foil/scrap + carbon air cathode (crushed charcoal/BBQ briquettes + steel wool current collector).
   - **Drawback**: Passivation/corrosion over time; add zincate suppressors (e.g., trace polyethylene glycol) if possible.

2. **Sodium Hydroxide (NaOH) — Similar to KOH but Slightly Lower Current**
   - Almost as good (conductivity ~0.4–0.5 S/cm at high conc.), cheaper/more available.
   - Drain cleaner ("caustic soda" at Bunnings ~$10/kg). Same mixing/safety as KOH.
   - Current: 80–90% of KOH in tests; still high for DIY (200–400 mA/cm² peaks).

3. **Salt Water (NaCl Saturated) — Safest & Cheapest, Decent Current**
   - Saturated table salt (~300 g/L water) — zero cost beyond salt.
   - Current: Lower than alkaline (50–200 mA/cm² typical), but videos show 300–500 mA in Al-air with large area/activated carbon cathode + fan for air flow.
   - Ultra-safe: No caustic burns, no strong fumes. Great starter.
   - Boost: Add vinegar (acetic acid) trace or H₂O₂ (pharmacy) for 20–50% more current by breaking oxide layers.

4. **Other Mild Options (Lower Current but Very Safe)**
   - Vinegar + salt: Mild, low current (~50–150 mA).
   - Epsom salts (MgSO₄) + sea salt: Some hobby builds claim good stability, moderate current.
   - ZnSO₄ (zinc sulfate from pool/fertilizer shops): For zinc-ion rechargeable, but lower power than metal-air.

### Quick Recommendations for Your DIY EV
- **For max current/power** (best chance at usable torque/speed in a small EV prototype): Use **KOH or NaOH alkaline** (20–30% conc.) with:
  - Zinc-air: Zinc plate/foil anode, carbon/MnO₂ air cathode (from crushed alkaline batteries).
  - Al-air: Aluminum foil/scrap anode (higher bursts), same cathode.
  - Large electrode area (multiple plates), forced air (small fan), flowing electrolyte if possible (peristaltic pump).
- **Realistic DIY currents**: 0.5–2 A per small cell (5–10 cm²) continuous; stack 10–20 cells for 12–24 V pack → 10–40 A total possible with optimization (enough for go-kart creep/acceleration).
- **Safety first for EV use**:
  - Ventilate (H₂ gas in alkaline).
  - Plastic enclosures (no metal shorts).
  - Fuses, BMS-like monitoring.
  - Start static (no flow), add pumps later.
